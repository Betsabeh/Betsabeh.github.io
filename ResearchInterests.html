My Research

Drug-target continuous binding affinity prediction using multiple sources of information

This work focous on predicting drug-target binding affinity (DTBA) using multiple sources of information. Unlike previous studies that typically rely on a single similarity source, our approach integrates various drug-drug and target-target similarities, considering all combinations as pairwise similarities. A k-nearest Neighbors (k-NN) algorithm identifies similar drug-protein pairs, and their binding affinities and similarities are used as features in a Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM) regression model. Our key contributions include: (1) leveraging multiple, combined similarity sources; (2) incorporating both neighborhood information and binding affinities as features; and (3) employing a GBM-based method to effectively integrate these diverse data sources and handle both regression and information fusion. This approach significantly improves prediction accuracy, particularly when predicting the binding affinity of new drugs and proteins, outperforming existing state-of-the-art methods. The method is robust to the inclusion of less informative similarity sources, maintaining performance even with increased data heterogeneity.
